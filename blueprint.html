The diagnosis: it isn't your teaching.
It's who's choosing the topic.

Your production quality is already high — the standards file is more rigorous than most publishers'. The problem is structural, and it's worth naming precisely before fixing it.

What the trial lesson promises

Six months from now: series without subtitles. Speaking up in a meeting without rehearsing. Telling your story and getting the job. Handling any airport. A transformation, with a destination.

It also names the student's real pain: "I understand English, but the words don't come out."

What the AEF-built lesson delivers

"Fasten your seat belts." Airport announcements. Match the word to the definition. Beautifully built — but the book picked the topic, so the lesson can only ever be as interesting as the unit it came from.

And crucially: nothing in it points at the six-month destination.

The gap, in one sentence You sell a destination and deliver a syllabus. Students feel the difference even when they can't articulate it — and it shows up as churn around lesson 8, when novelty fades and progress isn't visible yet.

The three fixes this blueprint makes

1 · Invert the hierarchy

The can-do goal picks the topic. Grammar rides underneath as a hidden spine. AEF becomes your private coverage checklist — never something the student sees.

2 · Make progress audible

Every lesson ends with a 60-second recording. At level end, you play lesson 1 next to lesson 30. Nothing else you can do sells a renewal as hard as a student hearing their own progress.

3 · Replace exercises with mechanics

Gap-fills check knowledge. Games force production under mild pressure — which is exactly the muscle "the words don't come out" is missing. 24 new mechanics are specced below.

On the AEF books You keep them — privately. Every level below lists its grammar spine, sequenced to match AEF's CEFR progression so no structure is skipped and a student transferring in or out lands cleanly. The books stay on your shelf as an audit tool. They never appear in a slide, a homework, or a student's mouth.
The signature

The 5-Beat Lesson

One repeatable 50-minute rhythm, used at every level from A1 to C1. Students learn the shape by lesson three and start arriving prepared for it. It also makes lessons dramatically faster for you to build — you're filling a known skeleton, not inventing a structure each time.

0–5 minHOOK

A game, a puzzle, a surprising fact. Never a quote. Secretly contains today's target language before it's taught.

5–15 minINPUT

Real English: a song, a scene, a short video, a story. Something a native actually made — not a textbook text.

15–30 minBUILD

Meaning → Form → Pronunciation, then controlled practice. Your existing CELTA sequence, unchanged.

30–45 minPLAY

The game beat. A mechanic from the bank that forces the target language out of the student's mouth under mild pressure.

45–50 minLAND

"You can now do X." Student records 60 seconds using today's language about their own life. Homework launched.

Why HOOK comes before INPUT

Your standards already say a warm-up should make the student use the target language before it's taught. The 5-Beat locks that in as structure rather than aspiration. By the time you name the grammar in BUILD, the student has already met it twice — once in play, once in real English. Naming it becomes a relief ("oh, that's what that was"), not an imposition.

Why LAND is non-negotiable

Five minutes, every lesson: state the can-do that was just unlocked, and capture a 60-second recording. This is what converts a nice lesson into visible progress. It also gives you a corpus of the student's own English to build later lessons from — the single richest source of personalised material you'll ever have.

Mission Complete lessons run a different shape Every fifth lesson replaces the 5 beats with: Warm-up (5) → Project presentation & feedback (20) → Can-Do Check (15) → Next mission trailer (10). It's a breather, a celebration, and an assessment in one — and it's where renewals get decided.

The A1 design problem — and the answer

A true beginner shares almost no language with you. The temptation is to fill the gap with translation, or with content built for children. Your trial lesson already solved this beautifully with the cognate wall — "you already know hundreds of English words." SAY IT scales that solution across the level: every A1 lesson opens with something the student can already partly do, so they never start from nothing. And because A1 speech is short, A1 activities are timed and countable — Beat the Clock, Sound Sprint, The Escalator — which turns tiny sentences into a visible score instead of a poverty.

The engine

24 activity mechanics

These are the PLAY beat. Every one is buildable as single-file HTML with no library, works one-on-one, and — as you asked — none of them is open role-play. Where a mechanic is role-play-adjacent, it's tightly guided and pointed only at the student ("What would you say if someone told you…"). Your existing mechanics — dice, wheel, MadLibs, bingo, scramble, breakout, myth-or-fact — all stay; these are additions.

Tier 1 — the six that change the most

If you build nothing else from this list, build these. Each one directly attacks "I understand English but the words don't come out."

🪜 The Escalator
A sentence starts as short as possible. Each round the student must add exactly one new element — an object, a time, a place, a reason, an opinion — without breaking it. A visual staircase fills one step at a time.
"I ate." → "I ate pizza." → "I ate pizza last night." → "I ate pizza last night because I was too tired to cook."
Levels: all — A1 does five steps, C1 does ten with subordination. Why it works: it makes sentence complexity visible and countable, so growth is felt inside the same lesson.
🎙️ Shadowing Studio
Native audio plays with the transcript marked for stress and linking. The student records themselves over it using the browser's own MediaRecorder — no plugin, no upload — then plays both back to back and hears the gap.
Levels: all. Why it works: the only pronunciation activity where the student, not the teacher, notices the error. Also the single most impressive thing a student can show a friend.
🔊 Connected Speech Decoder
A fast native clip plays. The student types what they actually heard, then reveals the real words. "Whaddaya wanna do?" → "What do you want to do?" Scored out of ten, replayable at half speed.
Levels: A2–C1. Why it works: it delivers directly on the promise you already make in the trial lesson — and it's the fix for "native speakers talk too fast."
🎚️ The Register Dial
One message, one slider. Drag from 1 (texting a friend) to 5 (writing to a regulator) and the sentence on screen morphs. Then the slider is emptied and the student produces each level themselves.
Levels: B1–C1. Why it works: register is the last thing separating a good B2 from a C1, and almost nobody teaches it explicitly.
💰 Word Auction
The student starts with 100 points and sees ten sentences, some correct, some not. They bet points on each according to how sure they are. Confident-and-wrong is expensive; hesitant-and-right is cheap.
Levels: A2–C1. Why it works: it separates knowing from guessing, which a normal Check Answers button can never do. It shows you exactly where false confidence lives.
⏱️ Beat the Clock
Sixty seconds on a visual bar. Produce as many as you can: things in this room, ways to say "very good", verbs that go with make, reasons you'd quit a job. The count is tapped in and the personal best is remembered.
Levels: all. Why it works: mild time pressure is what forces retrieval, and a personal best turns fluency into a score the student wants to beat.

Tier 2 — the rotation

Use these to keep the PLAY beat unpredictable. Your standards already say never to repeat a warm-up format three lessons running — the same rule applies here.

🩺 Sentence Surgery
A "sick" sentence appears on an operating table. The student clicks the word that's wrong, then chooses the cure from three options. The vital-signs bar goes green when it's healed.
All levels · gamified error correction
🎭 The Wrong Answer Game
Give the worst possible answer to a normal question — but it must be grammatically perfect. "How was your holiday?" → "I regret every second and I blame my mother."
A2–C1 · kills perfectionism anxiety
🧩 Odd One Out — But Justify
Four items. Any of them can be the odd one out, if you can justify it. There is no wrong answer, only a weak reason.
All levels · pure speaking, zero prep
😀 Emoji Translate
🛫😴🧳❓ → "I fell asleep on the plane and I think I lost my luggage." Student decodes, then builds their own for you to decode.
A1–B1 · forces full sentences
⏸️ The Pause Button
A scene plays and stops mid-beat. What is the next line? The student predicts, then it plays on. Score for meaning, not exact words.
A2–C1 · builds prediction, the core listening skill
🕵️ Guess My Word
The screen shows a word to one side only. The other asks yes/no questions to find it. Question counter visible; under seven is a win.
All levels · question formation, which students under-practice
🎛️ Where Do You Stand
A slider between two extremes ("money doesn't matter at all" ↔ "money is everything"). The student places themselves, then defends that exact position — not the extreme.
B1–C1 · nuance and hedging language
🤖 Fix the AI
"An AI wrote this paragraph and made six mistakes." The student hunts them. You write the errors deliberately, targeting the lesson's grammar.
A2–C1 · error correction with no ego attached
🗑️ Truth or Trash
Sentences sort into "real English people actually say" and "textbook English nobody says." "How do you do?" → trash.
B1–C1 · naturalness and register awareness
🧠 Memory Match
Classic flip-and-pair grid: collocation halves, phrasal verb and meaning, idiom and situation. Timed, with a personal best.
All levels · vocabulary consolidation
🔗 Chain Reaction
Each answer must start with the last letter of the previous one — and stay inside the topic. Timer per turn, three lives.
A1–B1 · lexical retrieval speed
🏆 Ranking Battle
Rank six items. Then you challenge the top choice and the student defends it, then the bottom, then you swap two and see whether they'll change their mind.
A2–C1 · opinion and concession language
🎧 Guess the Speaker
Three voices say the same sentence. Where are they from? How old? How do they feel? Reveal, then discuss what gave it away.
B1–C1 · accent exposure, listening for tone
🖼️ Caption This
An absurd photo. Write the funniest caption — but it must contain today's structure. Three attempts, the student picks the winner.
All levels · playful controlled practice
🛗 The Elevator
Thirty seconds, a random topic, no preparation, a live word counter running. The second attempt on the same topic always beats the first — which is the whole point.
B1–C1 · spontaneity under pressure
🎲 Story Cubes
Three random icons appear. Build a story containing all three, in the target tense. Roll again for a harder combination.
A2–B2 · narrative fluency
🔀 Two Truths and a Lie
The student writes three statements about their life in the target structure. You guess. Then you go, and they interrogate you.
All levels · personalised production and questions
💬 What Would You Say If…
The guided, student-only reply drill you asked for. A single line appears — "Someone tells you they've just been fired." The student responds. Three model responses reveal afterwards.
All levels · role-play's benefit, none of its awkwardness
Rotation rule — add this to your standards file A mechanic may not appear in two consecutive lessons, and each mission of five lessons must include at least one Tier 1 mechanic and at least three distinct Tier 2 mechanics. This is the difference between "the lessons have games" and "the lessons are unpredictable" — and unpredictability is what students actually experience as fun.

Between lessons

The AI Partner

Your students have unlimited access to a patient, fluent conversation partner, and almost none of them know how to use it. Give every lesson an AI Partner Card: one copy-paste prompt on the homework slide, with a copy button. It's the cheapest way to multiply practice hours without adding a minute of your time — and it's a real differentiator on your profile.

The rule that makes this work Every prompt ends by telling the AI to stay at the student's level and to correct only the target structure. Unconstrained AI corrects everything, overwhelms the student, and gets abandoned by week two. Constrained AI feels like a coach.
1 · The Interrogator every level · after any grammar point
Turns a new structure into twelve forced repetitions in ten minutes.
You are my English conversation partner. My level is [A1/A2/B1/B2/C1]. Ask me 12 questions, ONE AT A TIME, waiting for my answer each time. Every question must require me to use [the past simple] in my answer. Topic: [my last holiday]. After each answer: if I used [the past simple] incorrectly, show me the corrected sentence in bold and move on. Ignore every other kind of mistake completely. Do not explain grammar unless I ask. Keep your own language at my level.
2 · The Upgrader B1–C1 · vocabulary range
Attacks "I use the same few words again and again" — the complaint in your own trial-lesson diagnostic.
Here is something I wrote or said: [paste your text] Do three things: 1. Rewrite it so it sounds like a fluent native speaker, keeping my meaning and my personality. 2. List the 5 specific words or phrases you changed, and why yours is more natural. 3. Give me ONE of those five to use three times this week, and tell me exactly which situations to use it in. Do not make it more formal unless I asked. Do not add ideas that weren't mine.
3 · The Story Machine A1–B1 · comprehensible input
Generates unlimited level-appropriate reading built from exactly the words the student has.
Write me a story of about [150] words at [A2] level. Use ONLY these target words, and use each at least once: [paste this lesson's 10 words] Use the [past simple] throughout. Make it funny and give it a twist ending. Then ask me 5 comprehension questions, one at a time. Do not use any grammar above [A2].
4 · The Devil's Advocate B2–C1 · argument
Prepares a student to disagree in real time — the hardest B2 skill.
I am going to state an opinion. Your job is to disagree with me intelligently and keep pushing back for 6 exchanges, one at a time. Be respectful but genuinely hard to convince. Ask me for evidence. After the 6th exchange, stop and tell me: - the 3 strongest things I said, and why they worked - 3 phrases a native speaker would have used to concede a point before counter-attacking, which I did not use My level is [B2]. My opinion is: [state it]
5 · The Accent Coach all levels · pronunciation homework
Pairs with the Shadowing Studio in the lesson.
I am a [Brazilian] speaker learning English. My problem sound is [the TH sound]. Give me 8 sentences to practice it. Rules: - each sentence must contain the sound at least 3 times - they must be sentences a real person would actually say - order them from easiest to hardest Then explain, in one short paragraph, exactly what my tongue and lips should do, and what the most common mistake is for speakers of my language.
6 · The Slang Decoder B1–C1 · after any series or song homework
Turns the student's own Netflix habit into a lesson.
Here are 5 phrases I heard in [name the series or song]: [list them] For each one tell me: - what it actually means, in simple English - how formal or informal it is, from 1 to 5 - one situation where I could really use it - one situation where it would sound wrong or strange Then write a short dialogue that uses three of them naturally.
7 · The Mirror B2–C1 · self-analysis
Used with a transcript of the student's own recording. Confronting, and extremely effective.
This is a transcript of me speaking English for 3 minutes: [paste] Analyze it and tell me: - my 3 most repeated words or phrases (my verbal crutches) - the level of my vocabulary, as a CEFR estimate, with evidence - 3 sentences where I was correct but not natural, and what a native would say - ONE habit that, if I fixed it, would make the biggest difference Be honest and specific. Do not be encouraging for its own sake.
8 · The Simplifier all levels · confidence
For the student who freezes because they're trying to say something too complicated.
I want to say this idea in English but it's too difficult for my level: [the idea] Show me 3 ways to say it: 1. the way an A2 speaker would say it — simple, correct, clear 2. the way a B1 speaker would say it 3. the way a native would say it Then tell me which one I should practice this week, and why.
Between lessons

Homework people actually do

The test for every homework in this course: would a student do this even if nobody checked? Gap-fill worksheets fail that test. The six formats below pass it. Rotate them; never send the same type two weeks running.

🔥 The 7-Day Streak Card

Seven boxes, one per day, five minutes each, each a different micro-task built from this lesson. Monday: say your five new words out loud in sentences. Tuesday: text a friend one sentence in English. Wednesday: find today's structure in a song. The student ticks boxes and brings the card back. Five minutes daily beats forty minutes on Sunday — and the card makes that visible.

🎤 The Voice Note

Sixty seconds, recorded on WhatsApp, sent before the next lesson. One prompt, tied to the lesson's can-do. You reply with a 30-second voice note — not corrections, a reply, as a person. This is the highest-value homework in the entire course and it costs you three minutes per student per week.

📺 The Series Mission

One scene, three passes. First with no subtitles — what did you understand? Then with English subtitles — what did you miss? Then with subtitles in your language — what surprised you? Harvest five phrases and bring them. It turns the thing they were going to do anyway into study.

🎵 The Song Autopsy

Gap-filled lyrics, plus a hunt: find every contraction, every gonna and wanna, and one line that breaks a grammar rule on purpose. Then sing it once, badly, alone in the car. Say that last part out loud — it gives permission, and it works.

🤖 AI Sparring

Run this lesson's AI Partner Card, then screenshot the one exchange you're proudest of and one you found hard. Both get looked at next lesson. Practice hours multiply; your prep time doesn't.

📸 Wild English

Photograph three pieces of English you find in the real world this week — a sign, a t-shirt, a menu, an ad, a mistake. Bring them. Students become collectors, and a collector notices English everywhere between lessons.

Projects — one per mission, thirty across the course

The Mission Complete lesson is built around presenting a project. It's the visible artefact of five weeks of work — the thing a student shows their partner, keeps on their phone, and remembers three years later. Every project in the level tables above is recorded or filmed (so it can be compared later), personal (so it can't be copied), and finishable in a week (so it actually gets done).

Retention

Making progress impossible to miss

Students don't quit because lessons are bad. They quit because they can't feel movement. Three instruments fix that, and all three are cheap.

1 · The Fluency Log

Sixty seconds recorded at the end of every lesson, in the LAND beat, stored by date. Thirty lessons later you play lesson 1 and lesson 30 back to back. It takes ninety seconds and it's worth more than any certificate you could print.

2 · The Can-Do Check

At every Mission Complete: eight can-do statements from that mission. The student self-rates 1–5, then you rate them. Where the two disagree is the most useful conversation in the whole course — usually the student is underrating themselves, and showing them that is a retention event.

3 · The Proof

End of level: a ten-minute recorded performance against the level's thirty can-dos, plus a certificate naming the level ("SAY IT — completed") rather than a book. Sharable, screenshot-able, and it markets you for free.

Placement — you already have it

Your trial lesson is, structurally, a very good placement test that doesn't feel like one: the cognate wall establishes an A1 baseline, the pronunciation section identifies L1 interference, the "how often is this you?" grid diagnoses the blocker, and "in six months I want to…" captures the goal. One addition would complete it: a single spoken prompt at the end, recorded. That recording becomes entry zero of the Fluency Log, and gives you a defensible level placement instead of an impression.

The renewal conversation writes itself "Here's what you couldn't do five weeks ago. Here's the recording. Here's the next mission." No persuasion required — you're just reading out evidence.
Practical

How to build this without stopping work

A hundred and fifty lessons is a two-year build if you do it linearly — and you have students on Monday. So don't do it linearly.

What to add to your standards file

  • The 5-Beat structure as the default lesson slide order, replacing the current ten-section order — which stays valid as a component list.
  • The rotation rule — no mechanic twice in a row; one Tier-1 and three Tier-2 per mission.
  • The LAND beat is mandatory — every lesson ends with a can-do statement and a 60-second recording.
  • Every lesson declares its can-do goal on the cover slide, in the student's words, not grammatical terms. "By the end of today you can introduce yourself for a full minute" — not "Present simple: be."
  • Media-first rule — the INPUT beat may not use a text written for learners. If no authentic source fits the topic, the topic is wrong.
  • New mechanics get unique function names derived from the mechanic and slide id. Your existing collision rule already covers this, but it matters far more now that mechanics repeat across 150 files.
