Notice what the real ones all have in common: they give something away first. "I see what you mean." "That's fair." "You've got a point." Nobody in English walks straight into a disagreement — and the ones in the trash column fail not because they are ungrammatical, but because they are too direct to be survivable in a meeting.

BEAT 3 · BUILD Meaning — the three-part move

Every strong disagreement in English has three parts

1 · CONCEDE

Give them something real.
"You're right that it's expensive."

2 · PIVOT

One word turns the sentence.
"…but / although / that said…"

3 · ASSERT

Now say your thing, clearly.
"…doing nothing costs more."

You're right that it's expensive, but I'd argue doing nothing costs us more.

💭 Check your understanding
  • In that sentence, do I agree or disagree? (disagree — the second half is my real position)
  • Which half does the listener remember? (the second — English puts the point you want to win at the end)
  • If I swap the halves, does the meaning change? (yes — "It's expensive, but you're right" now concedes to them)

⚠️ This is the rule that matters: whatever comes after "but" is what you actually mean. Everything before it is the price you pay to be heard.

BEAT 3 · BUILD Form — your toolkit

Nine pivots, three strengths

Same job, different force. Click to hear each one.

Soft — you might still be persuaded

I take your point, but…
That's fair, although…
To some extent, yes — that said…

Firm — you have decided

Granted, but…
Admittedly, although…
Having said that…

Hard — you are drawing a line

I'm not sure I'd go that far.
With respect, that's not quite it.
I'd push back on that.

Two structures worth owning:

although / though + full clause  →  Although the data is limited, the pattern is clear.
whereas / while + contrast  →  Whereas the first study was small, the second covered ten thousand people.

⚠️ Although and but never appear in the same sentence. "Although it's late, but I'll come" is the single most common B2 error there is.

BEAT 3 · BUILD Pronunciation — the concession fall-rise

The tune tells them "there's a but coming"

Native speakers signal the pivot before they say it — with the melody. The concession half goes down, then up.

You're ↘right↗ about the cost,  but the timing ↘matters more.

Down-then-up on the concession = "I am not finished." A flat fall = "I agree, end of story."

Weak forms — these almost vanish

but  ·  that
to some extent
I'd argue that

Say these three at full speed

I take your point, but I'd argue the opposite.
Granted, although that's not the whole picture.
To some extent, yes. Having said that, I'm not convinced.

BEAT 4 · PLAY The Concession Ladder

🪜 One position. Five rounds. I attack every time.

Take a position on the statement below. Each round, I give you a counter-argument, and you must concede something real before you come back. You may never repeat a pivot phrase.

"Remote work is better for everybody."

1
Your position, in one sentence
I'd argue remote work is better for most people, most of the time.
2
I say: "But new employees learn nothing at home." → concede + come back
Granted, juniors do lose something — although that's an onboarding problem, not a location problem.
3
I say: "Teams lose their culture." → concede + come back
That's fair to an extent. Having said that…
4
I say: "Productivity data is mixed." → concede + come back
Admittedly the evidence isn't settled, but…
5
Final: restate your position, stronger than round 1
So while I'd accept it isn't universal, I'd still maintain…

BEAT 4 · PLAY What would you say?

🎲 Somebody says this to you. You have four seconds.

Roll the die. Answer immediately, out loud. You must concede first, then push back.

⚀
"Your English is very good — for a foreigner." "People who work from home are just lazy." "Learning languages is pointless now. We have AI." "Money genuinely does buy happiness." "Social media has been good for society overall." "Nobody needs a university degree anymore."
Teacher move: time them. Four seconds of silence is fine — six is a freeze. If they freeze, hand them the concession phrase and let them build from it. The goal is the reflex, not the argument.

HW2 Make it survivable

Rewrite each blunt sentence as concede → pivot → assert

Same meaning. Same strength. But now the other person keeps listening.

1   "That won't work."
I can see why you'd try it, but I don't think it'll hold up in practice. (example)

2   "You're wrong about the budget."

3   "We don't have time for this."

4   "That's a bad idea."

5   "Nobody agrees with you."

There is no single right answer — but a strong version always does three things. 2 "The budget's the right thing to focus on. That said, I read those numbers differently." 3 "Time's genuinely tight, granted — although I'd argue this is exactly what's eating the time." 4 "I take the thinking behind it. I'm not sure the execution gets us there." 5 "It's true I'm in a minority on this. Having said that, being outnumbered isn't the same as being wrong."
